# Strategy Framework

## Purpose

Turn research into a proprietary, credible and executable creative platform. The dossier must move from what is true now to what the prospect could do next.

## 1. Strategic chain

Build the argument in this order:

```text
current moment
→ existing capital
→ perception or communication gap
→ obvious shortcut to refuse
→ distinctive brand truth
→ human, cultural or business tension
→ strategic shift
→ active promise
→ creative platform
→ distinct routes or formats
→ first proof
```

Do not jump from a logo and a product photo directly to a tagline. Each arrow must be traceable in the diagnosis.

## 2. Diagnosis worksheet

Complete `strategy/diagnosis.md` with this structure.

### Why now?

- What changed recently?
- What business or communication moment makes action relevant?
- What would be lost by waiting?
- Which evidence IDs support this reading?

### Existing capital

- What already feels strong, credible or distinctive?
- Which products, services, people, places, processes or codes carry trust?
- What must the proposal preserve?
- Which current assets prove it?

### Perception gap

Compare underlying reality with visible communication:

| Reality | Current signal | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| What the company can do | What an outsider sees | What remains misunderstood or undervalued |

Write the gap as one sentence:

`The company possesses [credible capability or character], but its communication currently signals [narrower, generic or fragmented reading], which weakens [trust, memory, understanding, desirability or action].`

### Obvious shortcut

Identify the first category idea most people would use. Examples include luxury lifestyle, technical performance montage, generic heritage, founder hero, futuristic technology, Swiss clichés, abstract sustainability, dashboard UI, smiling team, or product-on-pedestal.

Explain:

- why the shortcut is tempting;
- what truth it flattens;
- what competitors already own it;
- what more specific reading should replace it.

Do not reject an authentic heritage code. Reject its literal, generic treatment.

### Distinctive truth

A usable truth is:

- supported by evidence;
- recognizable in product, service, place, process or behavior;
- difficult for a random competitor to claim unchanged;
- visually demonstrable;
- broad enough to support more than one execution.

### External tension

Choose a tension that matters outside the company. It may be human, cultural, operational or commercial. It must not be a fabricated trend.

Examples of tension forms:

- precision versus uncertainty;
- mastery versus conformity;
- complexity versus confidence;
- ubiquity versus memory;
- speed versus care;
- heritage versus present relevance;
- visibility versus credibility;
- consistency versus surprise.

### Strategic shift

Write three `from → to` shifts. Each must change how the company is read, not merely how a layout looks.

Strong forms:

- from technology to capability;
- from product detail to human consequence;
- from decorative heritage to active method;
- from isolated content to recognizable editorial system;
- from announcement to evidence;
- from category language to proprietary behavior.

## 3. Three core equations

Use the equation that best fits the prospect.

### Tension equation

```text
distinctive brand truth + unstable force in the world = active promise
```

Useful for product, luxury, culture, architecture and emotionally driven film platforms.

### Perception equation

```text
real capability - visible proof = trust gap
visible proof + clear narrative = confidence
```

Useful for technical, industrial, B2B, scientific and service companies.

### Cultural equation

```text
recognized code + unexpected behavior = memorable character
```

Useful for established consumer brands with strong awareness but predictable communication.

The equation is an internal tool. It may become a visual page, but do not force mathematical language into the client copy when it feels unnatural.

## 4. Candidate platform generation

Apply the brief's `angle_status` before generating candidates:

- `fixed`: the user supplied the strategic root. Generate at least three executions, phrasings or platform expressions that preserve that root. Do not replace it with a competing territory.
- `adaptable`: the user supplied a direction that may be sharpened. Generate at least three compatible interpretations inside that direction.
- `open`: no angle was supplied. Generate at least three candidates from genuinely different strategic readings.

Do not create three synonyms. Do not use scoring to overrule a fixed user angle; use it to select the most credible expression of that angle.

For each candidate record:

```markdown
## Candidate A: Platform phrase

- Evidence-backed truth:
- External tension:
- Strategic shift:
- Active promise:
- Why the phrase belongs here:
- Product/service code it activates:
- Visual world:
- Possible routes:
- Biggest risk:
- Evidence IDs:
```

Platform phrase guidelines:

- Prefer two to five words.
- Use an active idea, not an adjective stack.
- Make the phrase understandable without a paragraph, but richer after explanation.
- Avoid `redefining`, `beyond`, `future of`, `where X meets Y`, `excellence`, `innovation`, `passion`, `timeless`, `limitless`, `bold`, `crafted for`, `in motion`, and similar generic language unless the brand makes the phrase specific.
- Do not borrow the grammatical structure of an exemplar merely because it sounds premium.
- Test the phrase in the dossier language and any retained English version.

## 5. Platform scoring

Score every candidate from 0 to 5 on each criterion.

| Criterion | Question | Weight |
|---|---|---:|
| Evidence | Is the platform rooted in verified truth? | 3 |
| Ownership | Could a close competitor use it unchanged? | 3 |
| Strategic shift | Does it change the reading of the company? | 3 |
| Visual potential | Can it produce precise, non-generic images and layouts? | 2 |
| Route depth | Can it generate at least three distinct executions? | 2 |
| Business relevance | Does it serve the prospecting objective? | 2 |
| Verbal quality | Is it concise, clear and memorable? | 1 |
| Tonal fit | Does it sound like this brand, not an agency? | 2 |
| Risk control | Can it avoid category clichés and overclaiming? | 2 |

Calculate the weighted score out of 100. Reject any candidate scoring below 3 on Evidence, Ownership or Strategic shift, regardless of total.

Keep the scorecard in `strategy/diagnosis.md`. The highest score is not automatically selected when a fatal reputational, legal or production risk remains.

## 6. Platform operating system

Define the retained platform in `strategy/platform.md`.

### Core

- Platform phrase.
- One-sentence strategic definition.
- One-sentence active promise.
- Audience consequence.
- Evidence IDs.

### Four operating pillars

1. `Point of view`: what the company believes or enables.
2. `Code`: the real product, service, place, process or behavior that makes the idea visible.
3. `Territory`: scenes, subjects, themes and environments the platform may explore.
4. `Tone`: how the work should feel and what it must never become.

### Boundaries

Write:

- five things the platform is;
- five things it is not;
- three category clichés to reject;
- three proof types required to keep it credible.

### Message hierarchy

Define:

1. central message;
2. message for decision-makers;
3. message for customers or users;
4. message for partners or investors when relevant;
5. message for talent when relevant;
6. proof attached to each message.

### Channel roles

Do not prescribe every channel by default. Assign roles where useful:

- website: comprehension and trust;
- LinkedIn: expertise, progress and institutional proof;
- Instagram: culture, craft, people and visual memory;
- YouTube or video: explanation, story and scale;
- sales or investor material: decision-ready evidence;
- events or talks: embodied expertise.

The goal is a coherent proof system, not more content everywhere.

## 7. Route and film design

Create routes only after the platform is locked. Every route must express the same platform through a different tension, audience, product, format or moment.

### Route card

For each route define:

- name;
- one-line promise;
- specific subject or product;
- human or business tension;
- scene or evidence context;
- distinctive brand code;
- narrative turn;
- visual signature;
- final line;
- why it differs from the other routes.

Reject a route when only the product image or location changes.

### Film concept

Use this six-part frame:

1. `Scene`: immediate place, person and action.
2. `Pressure`: what becomes unstable, difficult or unresolved.
3. `Signal`: the precise brand, product or service code that enters the story.
4. `Shift`: what the protagonist understands or does differently.
5. `Resolution`: a credible change, never a magical product rescue.
6. `Signature`: final image and line.

The product or service may recentre, reveal, guide, prove or enable. It should not magically solve a human problem.

### Storyboard

When a duration is stated, assign timing. For a 20-second film, a useful starting rhythm is:

- 0–3 s: establish scene;
- 3–8 s: build pressure;
- 8–13 s: reveal signal;
- 13–17 s: shift or action;
- 17–20 s: resolution and lockup.

Adjust for the concept, but ensure the sum matches the promised duration. Label every frame with shot, action, sound and seconds.

## 8. Activation choice

Select one first proof. Answer:

- Why this activation?
- Why this subject, product, service or audience?
- Why now?
- What can it prove that a manifesto cannot?
- What assets and production conditions make it credible?
- How can the proof extend into other channels?

Score possible activations on:

- platform clarity;
- emotional or practical relevance;
- distinctiveness;
- evidence value;
- production feasibility;
- reuse potential;
- risk.

The first activation need not be a film. It may be a photographic proof system, editorial series, product education format, founder or expert presence, website narrative, event, publication or prototype.

## 9. Failure patterns

Reject or revise when:

- the insight could describe every competitor;
- the platform is only a prettier restatement of the product category;
- the angle depends on an unsupported claim about what the company wants;
- the creative shortcut is named but then reproduced in the images;
- the platform cannot generate three distinct routes;
- routes differ only cosmetically;
- the proposed film is a mood montage without tension or turn;
- the product behaves like magic;
- the activation appears before the strategic need is clear;
- the platform phrase copies the wording or metaphor of another dossier;
- the platform sounds like agency copy rather than a useful lens for the prospect.
